How breathing music works

From first breaths to deep calm — a short primer on the mechanisms behind the experience.

Start guide

Slow, even breathing is the body’s fastest path to calm. Stereo motion from left (inhale) to right (exhale) helps your body follow without effort.

Why left → right

Inhale cues draw attention inward; exhale lengthens and releases. Gentle left-to-right movement supports a balanced, embodied focus.

Vagus nerve & balance

Slow breathing increases vagal tone — the “calm-down” pathway — easing heart rate and blood pressure while shifting toward parasympathetic rest-and-restore.

Neurology & HRV

When you inhale, heart rate rises slightly; on exhale, it falls — a natural pattern called respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA). Longer, softer exhales typically amplify this beat-to-beat variability (HRV), a sign that your nervous system is flexible and resilient. Music-guided pacing makes this accessible and sustainable.

What is Breathing Music

Breathing Music began many years ago, after I experienced my first panic attack.

I realized that by changing my breathing pattern, I could prevent it from happening again. So I composed music with a steady, slow rhythm — a musical anchor to guide my breath when anxiety appeared. The concept worked so well that it became part of my daily life, helping me find calm whenever I needed it.

Some years later, someone close to me was struggling, and I created my first breathing music album, 5 Stille Moments. It was pure magic.

After that discovery, I began collaborating with, among others, Anette Aarsland, and have since created many hours of breathing music — for her exercises and for my own. The method is always the same:

  • Use headphones
  • Lie down or sit comfortably
  • Press play and let the music guide your breath
  • Inhale when the sound moves from the left side, exhale as it flows to the right
  • Breathe in through your nose, and vary your exhale naturally
  • Focus not on taking in more air, but on breathing slower
  • Let emotions come — tears, yawns, smiles, laughter — it’s all release
  • Practice gentle belly breathing — it takes time to strengthen the diaphragm
  • When finished, think of someone you care about and notice how you feel now

As a composer, husband, and father of three daughters, I often need to reset my mind and find focus again. Without this method, I’m not sure where life would have taken me.

The Future of Breathing Cycles

Once, Breathing Music was simply a way out of a personal crisis.

Today, it has grown into something much larger — a response to a shared human need. Breathing Cycles started as a simple tool for inner calm, but it has evolved into a project that explores how sound, breath, and technology can work together to restore balance in our lives.

It’s no longer just about slowing down; it’s about remembering how we are connected — body, mind, sound, and breath. The future of Breathing Cycles lies in building bridges between art and science, people and technology, the outer and the inner world.

Technology shouldn’t separate us from life — it should bring us closer to it. I believe that art, music, nature, and community are what will guide us forward. If this project can help even one person find a moment of peace, then it has already fulfilled its purpose.

How to practice

1) Get situated

Headphones on. Sit or lie comfortably. One hand on your belly; feel it rise and fall.

2) Pick a rhythm

Choose a playlist that matches your goal — reset, unwind, or sleep. Let the music set the pace.

3) Follow & grow

Breathe with the stereo movement. Start with 3–5 minutes and increase as it feels good.

FAQ

Is it free?

Yes. Spotify and YouTube are free to use. Donations are optional.

Is it safe?

Practice in calm settings. Don’t breathe-train while driving or doing tasks that require full attention. Pause if you feel dizzy.

Where should I start?

Pick your goal — quick reset, stress reduction, or sleep. Start short; extend naturally as your breath strengthens.
